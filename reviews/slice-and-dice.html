Slice and Dice (2021)

Developer: Tann
Publisher: Tann
Year of Release: 2021
Genre: Roguelike
Rating: 8.75/10

How do you rate an exercise? That’s the first question that comes to mind when talking about Slice and Dice. It’s a game less about narrative or flashy presentation and more about establishing a daily mental rhythm. At least, that’s how it’s been for me over the past six months. Morning coffee and Slice and Dice? Check. One quick game before bed? Check. Imagine boiling down a traditional JRPG battle to its essence: a back-and-forth clash of abilities between your party and the enemies, with light rogue-like progression layered on top. That’s Slice and Dice. Gameplay centers on choices—selecting which side of each die to play, deciding where to use abilities, choosing which items to employ, or whether to reroll. Each turn is a mix of dice rolls, strategic decisions, and hoping your health holds out until victory. The game loop is simple: a new battle, potential level-ups, and a fresh set of items. In many ways, it’s a distilled version of roguelike games. Stripped of sprawling maps or complex inventories, you can see the genre reduced to its core: decision-making under uncertainty, risk management, and incremental progress. In this form, these mechanics start to resemble classic brain exercises—puzzles and challenges designed to keep your mind sharp. The art direction matches this simplicity. The pixel art is clean, colorful, and pleasing without being distracting. I never really turned the sound or music on, since I play on my phone, but both are functional and unobtrusive. Everything feels designed to support quick, focused sessions rather than long, immersive storytelling. What makes it succeed is how it balances strategy and luck, wrapped in a streamlined, easy-to-control interface. The many modes and options mostly tweak the surface experience—they don’t fundamentally change the core loop—but they do add variety for repeat sessions. This is a game best approached as a mental side exercise rather than a full-on gaming event. Most players will get 5–10 hours of engaging play, but some of us—daily players included—might keep coming back indefinitely. Highly recommended for anyone who enjoys a thoughtful, low-pressure challenge with a dash of chance.
